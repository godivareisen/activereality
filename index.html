THE STRATEGY OF THE REAL (ACTIVE REALITY TRAINING)

The impossibility of rediscovering an absolute level of the real is of the same order as the impossibility of staging illusion. Illusion is no longer possible, because the real is no longer possible. It is the whole political problem of parody, of hypersimulation or offensive simulation, that is posed here.

In the wake of recent mass shootings (over 350 in 2015 alone), active shooter training is in high demand. Training institutes—often run by current and/or former members of law enforcement or the military—charge workplaces and schools up to thousands of dollars to deliver lectures or perform active shooter simulations in order to educate civilians on how to react in the event of a real active shooter or violent intruder attack.

Simulation is infinitely more dangerous because it always leaves open to supposition that, above and beyond its object, law and order themselves might be nothing but simulation. But the difficulty is proportional to the danger. How to feign a violation and put it to the test? Simulate a robbery in a large store: how to persuade security that it is a simulated robbery? There is no "objective" difference: the gestures, the signs are the same as for a real robbery, the signs do not lean to one side or another. To the established order they are always of the order of the real.

If people cannot rely on the police to save them, then how can they save themselves? Active shooter training programs ask their clients the hypothetical question: What would you do if someone walked in here and started shooting? The space of the hypothetical question maintains a working distance from reality—it's a safe space where people have the luxury of analyzing the possibility of threat. People can afford to think through their course of action without ever actually carrying it out.

Organize a fake holdup. Verify that your weapons are harmless, and take the most trustworthy hostage, so that no human life will be in danger (or one lapses into the criminal). Demand a ransom, and make it so that the operation creates as much commotion as possible--in short, remain close to the "truth," in order to test the reaction of the apparatus to a perfect simulacrum.

It is only in the moment that this question exits the hypothetical and enters into simulation that people understand that it is an attack on their own reality. Some active shooter training programs run simulations in which a shooter enters a room and the people inside are instructed to act according to their instincts. The human brain is calibrated to react by hiding from danger. But if that shooter was in fact intending to shoot (that is, if the shooter broke the simulation in the wrong direction) it would (in all likelihood) only be a matter of a few seconds until the people in the room were dead.

The Department of Homeland Security created a program called Run, Hide, Fight, in response to the demand for active shooter training. According to this program, the first (and best) option in the event of an attack is to run. Run where? To know where to run is to know how to break the conventional use paths of a given space. Is there always more than one way out? An unfamiliar space or a space with only one way out may as well be a death trap.

The challenge of simulation is never admitted by power. How can the simulation of virtue be punished? However, as such it is as serious as the simulation of crime.

If you can't run, you can't hide, and the police still haven't arrived, then the third option (and last resort) identified by the Department of Homeland Security is to fight back. In other words, do what the police would do if they were there to help you. But how are people expected to fight back? The strategy of repositioning oneself from the defensive to the offensive position, according to training institutes, changes the outcome of the event. In other words, the active shooter shifts from the offensive to the defensive—in the face of what?

Parody renders submission and transgression equivalent, and that is the most serious crime, because it cancels out the difference upon which the law is based. The established order can do nothing against it, because the law is a simulacrum of the second order, whereas simulation is of the third order, beyond true and false, beyond equivalences, beyond rational distinctions upon which the whole of the social and power depend. Does the average civilian have the physical, mental, or material means to fight back? Is introducing the unexpected enough to throw an active shooter off course? These training institutes speak as if active shooters operate according to a narrative than can be flipped. They expect that active shooters expect certain behaviors from their victims-to-be. Active shooter simulations accordingly exercise how people can subvert these expected behaviors in their own favor. If the active shooter performs off-script, what are people to do?
